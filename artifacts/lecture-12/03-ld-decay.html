LD Decay Explorer

Linkage disequilibrium is an autocorrelation between loci — how much knowing the allele at site A tells you about site B. Every generation of recombination is one round of smoothing; the expected correlation between two sites at distance d decays as exp(−2ct·d), where c is the recombination rate and t is the number of generations since the founding haplotype pool was laid down. Simulate a chromosome, extract the decay curve, and watch the characteristic distance shrink as either axis moves.

LD decay curve

binned mean r² (log-spaced distance bins) fitted exp(−2ct·d) half-max distance (r² = 0.5)

Every pair of simulated SNPs contributes one r² value at one pairwise distance; bin the distances log-uniformly and plot the within-bin mean. Fit a single-parameter exponential by ordinary least squares on the log of the mean r². The half-max distance is ln(2)/(2ct) — the characteristic distance at which correlations have fallen to 0.5.

Haplotype block & teaching notes

The heatmap reads like a triangular skyline: bright near the diagonal, fading as you move out. The brightness "halo" around the diagonal is the haplotype block. Its width matches the half-max distance on the left-hand curve — same number, two views. If the block fills the whole region, there has not been enough recombination (small c, small t). If the halo is a thin diagonal strip, recombination has erased nearly all long-range correlation.

Aha moment. Park at the default (c=1 cM/Mb, t=100) and note the half-max. Now multiply t by 10: the half-max shrinks by roughly 10×. Put t back, multiply c by 10: same thing. The decay constant is the product 2ct — neither axis alone fixes it.

Tiny gotcha. Low-frequency SNPs carry very noisy r². The simulation drops any SNP with MAF < 0.05 before binning; the "segregating SNPs" counter tells you how many survived.
